Inhabitants

Detailed care for the shrimp, snails, and fish in the tank — habitat, food, and what to expect.

Neocaridina shrimp

Neocaridina davidi

Habitat

Neocaridina shrimp do best in stable, planted tanks with plenty of surfaces to graze and hide. They like gentle flow and avoid strong currents.

  • Temperature: 18–29 °C (64–84 °F); mid-70s °F is ideal.
  • pH: 6.5–8.0. Avoid sudden swings.
  • Hardness: Moderate (e.g. 4–12 dGH). They need minerals for molting.
  • Tank setup: Plants, moss, and driftwood give cover and biofilm. They use the bottom and mid levels and appreciate hiding spots after molting.

Food

They are omnivores and graze constantly on biofilm and algae. Supplement with:

  • Quality shrimp pellets or sinking wafers (once daily or every other day, small amount).
  • Blanched vegetables (e.g. zucchini, spinach, cucumber) a few times a week; remove uneaten pieces.
  • Optional: occasional frozen foods (e.g. daphnia, brine shrimp) for variety.

In a mature planted tank they will also eat algae and decaying plant matter. Avoid overfeeding to keep water clean.

Behavior & care

They are peaceful and can be kept with small, non-aggressive fish and snails. Keep them in a group (e.g. 10+); they are more confident and active in numbers. Different color forms (cherry, blue, yellow, etc.) are the same species and will interbreed if mixed. They molt regularly; empty molts left in the tank are normal and can be left for them to eat for minerals.

Ramshorn snails

Planorbidae (e.g. Planorbella spp.)

Habitat

Ramshorn snails are very adaptable and do well in most community setups. They climb glass, plants, and decor and graze on surfaces.

  • Temperature: Comfortable in typical tropical range (e.g. 20–28 °C / 68–82 °F).
  • pH: Prefer neutral to slightly alkaline (around 7.0–8.0) for strong shells.
  • Hardness: Adequate calcium (GH) is important for shell health; very soft water can lead to thin or pitted shells.
  • Tank setup: No special requirements. They use all areas — glass, substrate, plants, and driftwood.

Food

They are scavengers and algae grazers. They will eat:

  • Soft algae on glass, plants, and decor.
  • Leftover fish and shrimp food, decaying plant matter, and detritus.
  • Blanched vegetables (zucchini, cucumber, spinach) if you want to supplement.
  • Optional: sinking pellets or algae wafers occasionally.

In a well-fed community tank they often need no dedicated feeding. Overfeeding is the main cause of population booms — reduce excess food to keep numbers in check.

Behavior & care

They are peaceful and harmless to plants (they may graze on dying leaves). They reproduce readily and lay small clutches of eggs on glass and plants. To limit population: avoid overfeeding, remove egg clutches if desired, and keep tank clean. They are useful clean-up crew and help break down waste.

Fish

Two species share the tank: Otocinclus (bottom) and Kubotai green rasboras (mid-water).

Otocinclus Otocinclus spp.

Habitat

  • Temperature: 22–26 °C (72–79 °F).
  • pH: 6.0–7.5. Stable, mature tanks are best.
  • Hardness: Soft to moderate (e.g. 2–12 dGH).
  • Tank setup: They need algae and biofilm; a mature, planted tank with driftwood and some algae growth is ideal. Gentle flow; avoid strong currents. Keep in a group (e.g. 6+).

Food

They are algae and biofilm grazers. In a new or very clean tank there may not be enough natural food, so supplement with:

  • Blanched vegetables (zucchini, cucumber, spinach) — leave in for a few hours.
  • Algae wafers or sinking pellets with vegetable content.
  • Repashy Soilent Green or similar gel foods if available.

They graze throughout the day. If they are constantly on the glass or plants, they are feeding; if the tank has little algae, offer supplemental foods regularly. They are peaceful and safe with shrimp and snails.

Kubotai green rasbora Microdevario kubotai

Habitat

  • Temperature: 22–28 °C (72–82 °F).
  • pH: 6.0–7.5. Soft to moderate water preferred.
  • Tank setup: Planted tank with open mid-water swimming space and some cover. They school; keep at least 8–10, preferably more. Gentle to moderate flow.

Food

They are omnivores and accept a wide range of foods:

  • Small pellets or micro pellets, and fine flakes (once or twice daily, small pinch).
  • Frozen foods: daphnia, brine shrimp, cyclops — good for variety and color.
  • They feed in the mid-water; ensure food reaches them and doesn’t all get taken by bottom feeders. Sinking pellets are fine if they catch them as they fall.

They are peaceful schooling fish and do not bother shrimp or snails when well fed. They are most active and colorful in a group.
