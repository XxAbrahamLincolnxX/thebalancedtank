The Nitrogen Cycle & Tank Basics

A healthy aquarium depends on the nitrogen cycle, setting up the environment before adding life, and testing the water regularly.

What is the nitrogen cycle?

In a closed aquarium, fish waste, uneaten food, and plant matter produce ammonia, which is toxic to fish. The nitrogen cycle is the process that turns that ammonia into less harmful compounds.

Beneficial bacteria that grow in the filter and on surfaces in the tank do the work:

  1. Ammonia (NH₃) — from waste and decay — is converted to nitrite (NO₂⁻) by one group of bacteria.
  2. Nitrite is still toxic. A second group of bacteria converts it to nitrate (NO₃⁻).
  3. Nitrate is much less harmful at low levels. You keep it in check with regular water changes and, in planted tanks, plants use some of it as fertilizer.

Until these bacteria colonies are established, ammonia and nitrite can spike and harm or kill fish. That’s why “cycling” the tank before adding livestock is so important.

Set up the environment before adding living creatures

Adding fish or other animals to a brand‑new tank is risky. The filter and surfaces don’t yet have enough beneficial bacteria to handle waste, so ammonia and nitrite can rise quickly and poison the inhabitants.

Cycling means running the tank (with a source of ammonia, such as a small amount of fish food or pure ammonia) for several weeks so that:

  • Bacteria that convert ammonia → nitrite can grow.
  • Bacteria that convert nitrite → nitrate can grow.
  • You see ammonia and nitrite rise, then fall to zero (or near zero), with nitrate present — that’s when the cycle is established.

Only after the cycle is complete and you’ve confirmed with a test kit should you add fish or other livestock. Doing this first keeps the environment safe and reduces stress and loss.

Test the water regularly

Even in a cycled tank, things can change: overfeeding, new fish, or a filter problem can cause ammonia or nitrite to appear again. Regular testing lets you catch problems early and keep the environment as healthy as possible.

Test at least:

  • Ammonia — should be 0 (or undetectable).
  • Nitrite — should be 0 (or undetectable).
  • Nitrate — keep it low (e.g. under 20–40 ppm depending on stock); water changes are the main way to reduce it.

During cycling, test every few days so you can see the cycle progress. In an established tank, testing weekly (or after any change) is a good habit. A simple liquid test kit is accurate and lasts a long time — it’s one of the best investments for your tank’s health.
